Question 1 of 8 — Current Tools
How does your company currently manage procurement intake and approval workflows?
Intake is where procurement requests enter the system. Approval is where they get authorised. Most companies handle this in email, spreadsheets, or a legacy ERP that was never designed for it.
Email and spreadsheets only. No formal intake system.
A legacy ERP or P2P tool (Coupa, SAP Ariba, Oracle). Works but complex and slow.
A mix of tools. Some teams use structured intake, others don't.
A modern orchestration platform with AI-driven intake and approval automation.
Question 2 of 8 — Spend Visibility
How much visibility does your finance team have over upcoming spend before it's committed?
Maverick spend — purchases made outside the procurement process — accounts for 20–40% of B2B spend at companies without intake orchestration. The CFO finds out after the invoice arrives.
Low. Finance sees spend when invoices arrive, not when it's requested.
Partial. Some categories are tracked but significant maverick spend exists.
Moderate. Most spend goes through a process but exceptions are common.
Strong. Finance has real-time visibility across all spend categories before commitment.
Question 3 of 8 — Approval Workflow
How long does a typical procurement approval take from request to sign-off?
At most companies without intake automation, approvals are tracked in email threads. Requests get lost. Stakeholders chase over Slack. Finance can't see what's pending. Average time-to-approval is measured in weeks, not days.
2+ weeks. Approvals are tracked informally and often get stuck.
5–10 days. A process exists but it's slow and manual.
2–5 days. Reasonably structured but still room to improve.
Under 2 days. Automated workflows route requests to the right approver instantly.
Question 4 of 8 — Supplier Management
How does your company manage its supplier relationships and contract renewals?
Unmanaged renewals are one of the biggest sources of wasted spend. Software auto-renews. Contracts roll over at higher rates. Nobody notices until the invoice hits the P&L. Supplier relationship management is where procurement teams find the most immediate savings.
No system. Renewals are managed by whoever remembers. Contracts live in email.
A spreadsheet or shared doc. Better than nothing but hard to scale.
A contract management tool or CLM. Renewals are flagged but manually managed.
Automated renewal tracking with supplier health scoring and negotiation workflows.
Question 5 of 8 — ERP Integration
How well does your procurement process integrate with your existing systems?
The "rip and replace" problem: most enterprise procurement tools require replacing your ERP or P2P system. An orchestration layer sits on top of what you have, connecting the systems you already paid for. The difference is months of implementation vs weeks.
No integration. Procurement and finance use separate tools with no data flow.
Partial. Some systems connect but data reconciliation is manual.
Moderate. Key systems are connected but procurement remains siloed from finance.
Strong. Procurement sits as an orchestration layer across our ERP, P2P, and contract tools.
Question 6 of 8 — AI in Procurement
How is AI currently used in your procurement operations?
AI in procurement ranges from bolted-on features (OCR, basic automation) to genuinely AI-native platforms built from the ground up. The distinction matters: an AI-native platform routes requests intelligently, identifies patterns in spend, and flags risks. Bolted-on AI is a search bar and a few macros.
No AI in procurement. Everything is manual.
Basic automation (rules-based). Not AI — just if/then logic.
AI features bolted onto a legacy platform. Works partially.
AI-native platform. Intelligent routing, spend pattern analysis, risk flagging.
Question 7 of 8 — Business Expansion
Is your company expanding into new markets or geographies in the next 12 months?
US expansion is the most common trigger for a procurement infrastructure overhaul. New markets mean new vendors, new compliance requirements, and procurement teams that don't have the same institutional knowledge as the home market. The pressure to standardise procurement is highest when the business is scaling fast.
No active expansion plans.
Considering expansion but no confirmed plans.
Active expansion — 1 to 2 new markets in the next 12 months.
Rapid expansion — multiple markets, fast headcount growth, procurement under pressure.
Question 8 of 8 — Finance Team Pain
How much time does your finance team spend on procurement-related admin per week?
Procurement admin for finance includes: chasing approvals, reconciling invoices, managing renewal dates, responding to vendor queries, and producing spend reports. Companies with effective intake orchestration reduce this by 60 to 80 percent. That's time the finance team gets back for actual analysis.
Less than 5 hours per week. Procurement runs smoothly.
5 to 10 hours per week. Manageable but noticeable.
10 to 20 hours per week. A real time sink.
20+ hours per week. Procurement admin is crowding out finance strategy work.
